The Principal Upanishads

There are over 200 Upanishads, but 108 are considered traditionally important, and out of those, 10 or 11 are considered the "Mukhya" (Principal) Upanishads, commented upon by ancient scholars.

  • Isha: Focuses on the concept of renunciation and the omnipresence of the Divine.
  • Kena: Explores the nature of Brahman as the absolute power behind the universe and the human mind.
  • Katha: The famous dialogue between the boy Nachiketa and Yama (Death) about the immortality of the soul.
  • Prashna: Structured as six questions from students to a sage, discussing cosmology and life breath (Prana).
  • Mundaka: Distinguishes between higher knowledge (spiritual) and lower knowledge (intellectual/ritual).
  • Mandukya: The briefest Upanishad, exploring the four states of consciousness mapping to the syllable "AUM".
  • Taittiriya: Discusses the five sheaths (Koshas) of the human personality.
  • Aitareya: Explores the creation of the universe and the nature of the self.
  • Chandogya & Brihadaranyaka: The two oldest and largest, establishing the core philosophy of Atman (Self) and Brahman (Cosmic Reality).

Sanskrit Glossary

A quick reference guide to the ancient terminology used throughout the book.

  • Atman: The true, eternal inner self or soul.
  • Brahman: The ultimate, infinite cosmic reality; the structural fabric of the universe.
  • Dharma: Cosmic law, order, duty, and righteousness.
  • Dhyana: Meditation and profound concentration.
  • Karma: Action, work, or deed, and the principle of cause and effect.
  • Koshas: The five layered "sheaths" of the human personality.
  • Maya: The illusion of the phenomenal, ever-changing material world.
  • Prana: The vital, cosmic life force or breath.
  • Vairagya: Detachment from the emotional bondage of the material world.
